The shape of attention

We talk about attention as if it were a spotlight — a single beam we point at one thing and then another. But attention is less like a spotlight than like a posture: a way of holding the whole body of the mind toward the world. The metaphor we choose quietly decides what we think is possible to fix.

There is a popular idea that attention is a renewable resource, depleted by use and restored by rest. It is a comforting picture, and partly true. Yet the deepest kinds of attention seem to deepen the more they are used, not less — the practiced reader does not tire of reading the way the novice does. The economy of a skill is not the economy of a battery.

What we call focus is often just the absence of competing invitations. Remove the notifications, the open tabs, the half-formed intentions, and concentration arrives almost on its own. We have been treating a problem of subtraction as a problem of will.

The history of reading is partly a history of furniture and light: the lectern, the carrel, the reading lamp. Every era invents the architecture its attention requires, and ours is only beginning to build for the screen.

So the question is not how to force the mind to attend, but how to arrange a world worth attending to. A page, after all, is a kind of room. The work is to make the room quiet enough that thinking can happen in it.
